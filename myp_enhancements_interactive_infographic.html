Milestones

1. When Do the Changes Happen?

The rollout spans multiple years to ensure schools can adapt gracefully to the new pedagogical patterns.

2027

IB Exchange Launches

A brand-new, interactive digital platform replaces static PDF guides. New subject guides launch with dynamic, adaptive resources and model units.

2028

First Teaching

Schools officially begin implementing the enhanced curriculum framework. New simplified unit planners, updated assessment criteria, and the flexible Integrated Core go live.

2030

First eAssessments

First formal digital assessments under the updated 3-criterion structure. Interactive, media-rich questions debut to evaluate student learning under the fresh paradigms.

2. Four Areas of Change

The program upgrades are divided into four fundamental sections designed to enhance clarity, flexibility, and direct utility for teachers.

📘

Enhanced Programme Guidance

Clearer global rules with structural flexibility in early years (MYP 1–3), redesigned layouts, and condensed global program aims.

Focus: Overall Structure
📗

Enhanced Subject Guidance

Actionable pedagogical suggestions. Shifts from abstract formulas to concrete strategies, making unit planning easier and intuitive.

Focus: Teaching Practice
📊

Enhanced Assessment Model

Transitioning from 4 to 3 main assessment criteria. Introduces student-centered "I Can" statements and reduced evaluation strands.

Focus: Assessment Clarity
🌐

IB Exchange interactive portal replaces hundreds of static pages. Teachers gain a dynamic repository of resources and planners.

Pedagogy

3. The Integrated Core

The core pillars remain mandatory, but the compliance burden is stripped away. The IB shifts from strict checklists to empowering localized designs.

Interdisciplinary Learning

IDU: Formats Unleashed

Highly Flexible

The rigid requirement to block a full traditional, multi-week interdisciplinary unit inside timetables has been dismantled. Schools can design flexible interdisciplinary setups that match their operational environment.

What remains mandatory:

Conduct and formally assess at least 1 interdisciplinary learning experience per year using the IDU criteria.

What becomes flexible:

The design format! Choose off-timetable focus weeks, field-trip integrations, custom collaborative labs, or standard split-subject setups.

Skill Development

ATL: Simplified Planning

Zero Redundancy

The compliance fatigue of planning skills is minimized. Large, complex documentation tables mapping out extensive sub-categories are gone. ATL maps can now be embedded organically in general curriculum documentation.

5 Core Organizers Stand

The main clusters (Communication, Social, Self-management, Research, and Thinking) stay active as foundational guides.

Sub-Clusters Removed

The detailed sub-categories are completely retired to prevent checklist-style compliance planning by teachers.

Community Engagement

CE: From Action to Dialogue

Reflexive Shift

Transitioning away from the traditional checklist "Service as Action" toward genuine relationship building, dialogic interaction, and contextual reflexivity.

4 Condensed Objectives
  1. 1 Explore systems & understand personal agency roles.
  2. 2 Develop dialogue and organic community relations.
  3. 3 Engage in self-critical, reflective and reflexive actions.
  4. 4 Design and execute principled, ethical actions.
5 Forms of Engagement
Advocacy Action Research Social Entrepreneurship Participation Community-building
Self-Directed Projects

Community & Personal Projects

Streamlined Process

The Community Project (Year 3) has been simplified and redesigned to act as a supportive pathway toward the Personal Project (Year 5).

Community Project (Y3)

Formal criteria scoring is retired. Rubric pressure is replaced by showcasing organic artifacts like reflection journals, narratives, and final exhibitions.

Personal Project (Y5)

Maintains its solid, externally moderated guidance, but gains smoother preparation via the simplified structures in earlier years.

Inquiry

4. Six New MYP Themes

Replacing the old "Global Contexts." Themes provide culturally responsive, highly organic, and localized framings to construct units.

Theme 1

Growing through experiences and belonging

Explores personal identity, development, values, human relationships, and structural communities.

Theme 2

Connecting time, place and space

Focuses on history, environments, geography, lineages, local narratives, and universal connections.

Theme 3

Expressing diverse perspectives

Dives into cultural expressions, human beliefs, artistic structures, narratives, values, and diverse outlooks.

Theme 4

Exploring innovations and impacts

Examines technological developments, scientific breakthroughs, solutions, and their societal footprints.

Theme 5

Examining complexity in systems

Decodes organizational structures, ecological loops, economies, governmental policies, and global networks.

Theme 6

Shaping the future with hope

Encourages sustainable pathways, active agency, ethical leadership, and constructive planning.

Implementation Tip: During planning, the chosen Theme works alongside the Central Idea to spark lines of inquiry. The Theme frames the human and cultural space, while the Central Idea targets core understandings.

Evaluation

6. Assessment Architecture

The traditional four-criteria system is retired. Evaluation gets streamlined and localized with flexible choice paths.

Universal Evaluation Enhancements

  • 1
    Unified 3-Criterion Model

    All subject groups shift from 4 criteria to 3 comprehensive criteria: Investigating, Applying, and Evaluating.

  • 2
    Embedded Command Terms

    Action command definitions are integrated directly into level descriptors; no separate glossaries to memorize.

  • 3
    Asset-Based "I Can" Language

    Student-friendly success statements replace rigid deficits, promoting self-reflection and goal alignment.

  • 4
    Broader & Fewer Strands

    Rubrics are simplified by grouping previous strands, dramatically reducing grading overhead.

Flexible Assessment Pathways

To ease transition and match student maturity, schools have total autonomy to decide when their students are ready to move from general skill concepts to rigorous, subject-specific criteria.

Years 1–3 Option
Subject-Generic Criteria

Reduces overwhelm for early learners. Broadens focus to roughly 10 shared transferable criteria strands across subjects.

Years 4–5 Option
Subject-Specific Criteria

Prepares students for rigorous DP/CP pathways with explicit subject criteria expectations and strands.

Key Choice: The school leadership team holds full autonomy to map transition pathways that best fit their campus, timetables, and student profile readiness.
